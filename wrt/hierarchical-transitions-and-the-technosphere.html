What‚Äôs important to grasp is the invitation to conceptualize the sheer scale of the technosphere and the scope of what this planetary technological system is composed of, or what sort of component systems and processes might be thought to consitute its identity. From relatively simple products like office memos and fertilizer, to complex intergenerational socio-cultural forms like religious institutions, cities, and governments, the magnitudinous ambit of the technosphere is vast and totalizing, assimilating nearly all phenomena connected to the workings of contemporary human civilization, including the behaviour of human subjects themselves.

This leads directly to one of the most crucial insights generated by Haff‚Äôs account of large-scale technological networks: i.e. a shift in our thinking away from the ‚Äúnatural assumption that the technosphere is primarily a human-created and controlled system‚Äù and towards ‚Äúthe idea that the workings of modern humanity are a product of a system that operates beyond our control and that imposes its own requirements on human behaviour.‚Äù What could justify a claim like this? One simple idea underlying this view is the relationship between organization and constraint in dynamic systems. I'll expand on this once we get into the biology of hierarchic transitions, but the gist is that in order for a complex dynamic system to persist through time and space, most of the behaviour of its component parts and processes must be consistent with the system‚Äôs functioning. In other words, systemic consistency and robustness requires functional organization, which implies constraints on the degrees of freedom accessible to constituent components.

Once we're committed to conceiving of the technosphere as as a higher-level unity defined by organizational relations between coupled subsystems, we can begin to see how the interests of human agents, as subordinate elements of the macro-system, must be constrained to some extent so as to align with the properties and/or emergent principles of the complex dynamic system within which they are embedded and whose functioning they are reliant upon. Haff's 2014 text supplies us with some examples of what these constraints could be, ranging from "hard" and tangible constraints like architectural and infrastructural barriers, to "soft" and more abstract or normative regulations, such as incentives and/or deterrents for behaviour that supports or interferes with the functioning of a given host system. In an effort to illustrate more broadly the contingent relationship between species survival and a functional technosphere (thereby implying that contemporary human populations are beholden to the organizational constraints of large-scale technological systems) he also advances the following historical speculation: "the pre-Anthropocene, pre-technological ‚Ä¶ human population was perhaps 10 million" and ‚Äúthe ramp-up of technology from pre-history through, e.g., dynastic China, Rome and medieval Europe to today‚Äôs global technology has led to the expansion of this population to a level approaching 10 billion today ‚Ä¶ suggesting that on the order of 999 out of every 1000 humans owe their existence and their wellbeing to technological systems."

In the context of Haff‚Äôs scholarship, the emphasis on this quasi-autonomous property of the technosphere serves as a basis for unconventional investigations into how our networked technological systems, conceived of as a single emerging geological process, interfaces with other Anthropocene-related phenomena in a manner which understates human autonomy over the production and transformations of the built environment. After all, the Anthropocene etymologically denotes a new geological epoch where significant impacts on the planet's climate and ecosystems are the result of the activity of humankind, and therefore part of Haff‚Äôs contribution to that discourse is to invert our assumptions about the directionality of this causal relation.
